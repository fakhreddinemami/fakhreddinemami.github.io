<div class="project-detail__tag">Hierarchical honeycombs | optimization | experiments</div>

Honeycombs are widely used because they are light, efficient, and manufacturable, but their bending-dominated cell walls limit how much stiffness and strength can be achieved at very low density. Hierarchy offers a way to break that limitation by adding structure inside the structure.

In this work, a solid skin and triangular lattice core are coordinated into a hierarchical honeycomb architecture. Finite element modeling and machine learning are used to explore the design space, while the mechanics of global instability, local instability, material failure, and skin-core interface effects guide the optimization.

The study shows that careful coordination across length scales can produce multi-fold improvements in mechanical performance. Experiments on fabricated specimens validate the numerical predictions and demonstrate that hierarchy can turn a conventional honeycomb into a stronger and stiffer lightweight material.
